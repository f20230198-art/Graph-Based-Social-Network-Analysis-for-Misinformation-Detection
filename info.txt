Project Title
Graph-Based Social Network Analysis for Misinformation Detection
Abstract
The rapid spread of misinformation on social media platforms poses serious social and political risks. Traditional detection systems mainly analyze the textual content of posts, but misinformation often spreads through coordinated social interactions rather than through text alone. This project proposes a graph-based framework that models social media as a network of interacting users to detect misinformation. Users are represented as nodes, and interactions such as reposts, replies, or mentions form connections between them. By extracting network features such as community structures, influence patterns, and information propagation behavior, machine learning models can identify suspicious spreading patterns associated with misinformation campaigns. The system aims to improve detection accuracy by combining content and interaction patterns, enabling earlier identification of coordinated misinformation networks.

Project Idea in Simple Terms
Most fake news detection systems check:
What is written in the post?


This project instead asks:
How is the post spreading?


Who is spreading it?


Does the spread pattern look suspicious?


Misinformation often spreads through:
Coordinated groups


Bot-like accounts


Highly connected hubs


Sudden rapid resharing


So we analyze interaction patterns, not just text.

Key research gaps and limitations
Based on the GNN-focused survey and recent hybrid papers, several gaps align directly with your intended contributions.
Static vs dynamic graphs:
Most GNN fake-news models treat the interaction graph as static, even though misinformation cascades evolve over time; temporal dynamics are often reduced to simple features (e.g., time to first 100 retweets) rather than full dynamic graph modeling.
There is limited use of temporal GNNs or diffusion-aware architectures that explicitly model the sequence and timing of interactions, especially for cross-event generalization.​
Graph construction choices underexplored:
Many works build article–user or user–user graphs with heuristic edge definitions (e.g., any retweet/mention) and simple weights, with little ablation on how graph topology choices affect performance.
Trade-offs between different node types (users vs posts vs publishers), edge semantics (retweet vs reply vs co-engagement), and edge weighting schemes (frequency, recency, rumor-spread rate) remain under-studied.
Limited multimodal and multi-view integration:
Classic methods often use either content or network, or concatenate hand-crafted features, whereas recent hybrid GPT-GNN and multimodal GNN models show that explicitly combining text embeddings with graph structure yields sizable gains.
However, designs for principled fusion (late vs early fusion, attention over modalities) and explainable cross-modal reasoning are still emergent research topics.​
Generalization across topics/events and domains:
Many fake news models are trained and tested within a single domain (e.g., US politics or COVID-19), which risks overfitting to topical cues; the GNN survey highlights concerns about poor generalization and domain shift.​
Datasets like MiDe22 explicitly span multiple events (COVID-19, Russia–Ukraine war, refugees, etc.), but cross-event and cross-language evaluations are still relatively sparse.​
Label and dataset limitations:
Labeling often depends on fact-checker platforms (PolitiFact, Snopes, etc.), creating label bias and limited coverage of everyday misinformation.
Graph datasets with full propagation trees and rich user metadata (e.g., FibVID, FakeNewsNet) remain relatively few, with platform policy changes (e.g., API restrictions) making updated large-scale graph collection challenging.​
Explainability and coordinated behavior detection:
While some models like multimodal GNNs and GPT-GNN frameworks begin to provide interpretable node/edge attributions, most GNN-based fake news detectors still act as black boxes.
Coordinated inauthentic behavior (e.g., botnets, astroturfing) is usually not explicitly modeled as higher-order structures (e.g., dense near-cliques, repeated co-retweet patterns), leaving room for richer graph motif and subgraph pattern analysis.

1. Graph-Theory SEIR Health Misinformation in Aging Populations​
Weak empirical grounding of the model parameters:
The SEIR transmission probabilities are said to be “calibrated with Bayesian inference,” but the paper does not specify priors, likelihoods, or validation metrics, nor does it quantify uncertainty, leaving the realism of the diffusion dynamics under-argued.​
Insufficient dataset transparency and demographic validation:
The Twitter/Facebook dataset for “aging” users is not described in terms of size, timeframe, labeling pipeline, or how age was inferred, so it is unclear how well the graphs actually represent older adults and how biased or noisy the data may be.​
Limited link between simulation outputs and real interventions:
The paper simulates interventions (removing 5–10% of central nodes, literacy campaigns) but does not test these against real-world platform policies, sociotechnical constraints, or adversarial responses, leaving a large gap between model-based recommendations and operational deployment.​
Under-modeled heterogeneity in older adults and content types:
Older adults are treated via generic parameters (trust, confirmation bias, fact-check frequency), with no modeling of subgroups by culture, language, comorbidities, or cognitive profiles, nor is health misinformation stratified into categories with distinct dynamics (acute epidemics vs chronic disease myths etc.).​
Ethics, privacy, and governance largely unaddressed:
The work relies on large-scale social-media data about a vulnerable group but does not engage with ethical review, data-protection constraints, or potential harms of “targeting” high-risk clusters, leaving a clear governance gap.​

2. Knowledge-Based Fake News Detection with BERT + (Planned) GCN/GNN​
Core GCN/GNN contribution is not empirically realized:
Although the title and methods emphasize combining BERT with GCN/GNN on a knowledge graph, the reported experiments only compare BERT and GPT‑3 on a knowledge base; no actual GCN/GNN results or end-to-end graph-enhanced classifier are presented.​
Sparse description and release of the Twitter dataset:
The Twitter corpus used to build the knowledge base is not characterized (N tweets, collection window, labeling protocol, topic balance), and is only available “on request,” which limits reproducibility and assessment of sampling and annotation bias.​
No ablation to justify SPO, sentiment, and topic components:
SPO triples, SPO sentiment polarity, SPO occurrence, and LDA topics are added as knowledge-base attributes, but there is no systematic ablation to quantify how much each component contributes relative to plain BERT or GPT‑3.​
Multimodal (image + text) side is only sketched:
ResNet50-based image features and multimodal fusion are described conceptually, yet final experiments and metrics are purely textual; the model’s claims about Twitter images and multimodality remain untested.​
Scalability and streaming robustness not addressed:
The framework is designed around offline knowledge-graph construction; there is no analysis of how it would handle high-velocity Twitter streams, frequent concept drift, adversarial perturbations, or near-real-time latency constraints.​

3. Contextual + Sentiment + Social-Credibility Hybrid Fake News Detector​
Satire, borderline, and nuanced content remain problematic:
The model frequently misclassifies satire (e.g., The Onion, Babylon Bee) and ambiguous news as fake due to reliance on exaggerated style and high sentiment polarity, indicating a gap in modeling pragmatic cues, intent, and genre distinctions.​
Multimodal misinformation (images, videos, memes) largely unaddressed:
The system is text- and sentiment-centric with graph-based social features; it explicitly acknowledges that it cannot reliably handle image- and video-driven misinformation or meme-based campaigns, and calls for multimodal deep learning as future work.​
Limited exploration of explainability and human interpretability:
The architecture is a complex ensemble (transformers, CNN, LSTM, GNN, RF, XGBoost), but no XAI tooling or explanation pipeline is implemented; the need for transparent, traceable decisions is deferred to future research.​
Generality across domains, languages, and platforms not validated:
Experiments are on LIAR, FakeNewsNet, and BuzzFeedNews (mostly English and news-centric); performance on other domains (health, finance), non-English languages, and different platform dynamics is not studied.​
Deployment, robustness, and adversarial considerations are thin:
There is no analysis of inference latency, scalability, or vulnerability to adversarial manipulation (e.g., style obfuscation, bot coordination changes), even though the model relies heavily on both stylistic and social-graph features.

Potential Database:

FibVID: Fake news diffusion dataset during the COVID-19 infodemic (Twitter)
Description: 1,353 news claims (True/False from Snopes, PolitiFact, etc.) with 221,253 related tweets/retweets from 144,741 users, including tweet IDs, user IDs (pseudonymized), propagation depths, and claim labels.​
Graph aspects: Allows building claim-level diffusion trees, user–user retweet graphs, and user–claim bipartite graphs with temporal information.
Link: 
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9759652/
​
MiDe22: Multi-Event Tweet Dataset for Misinformation Detection
Description: 10,348 tweets (English and Turkish) labeled as True/False/Other across 40 events (COVID-19, Russia–Ukraine war, refugees, etc.), plus user engagements (likes, replies, retweets, quotes) and media indicators.​
Graph aspects: Engagement counts can be expanded into user–tweet and user–user interaction graphs if you fetch additional metadata via APIs or use provided IDs.
Link: 
https://arxiv.org/html/2210.05401v2

Evaluation metrics and baselines
Metrics commonly used
Empirical studies in GNN-based and propagation-based fake news detection report:
Accuracy: Widely reported; hybrid and GNN-based methods often show gains of several percentage points over traditional baselines (e.g., ~97% accuracy for GNN hybrids in recent work).
F1 score (macro / weighted): ATA-GNN and hybrid GNN models emphasize F1 to capture performance on both fake and real classes; reported F1 around 90–96% on Twitter15/16, PHEME, FakeNewsNet variants.
ROC AUC: Used to assess ranking quality; ATA-GNN reports high AUC (93–97%) against prior graph-based baselines.​
Precision and recall for fake class: Often reported where fake content is the minority or of primary interest; some models highlight precision improvements (e.g., ATA-GNN achieving high precision on PHEME) and balanced recall.
For rare or emerging events: AUCPR (area under precision–recall curve) and per-event F1 are recommended in survey discussions, especially when fake/news imbalance is severe.​
For misinformation campaigns (rare coordinated behavior), explicitly reporting:
Per-class precision/recall for “campaign-level” labels if you define them, and
Detection delay (time to flag relative to first post)
would be valuable extensions beyond standard metrics.
Evaluation matrix could include:
Text-only (e.g., BERT or RoBERTa fine-tuned on posts).
Network-only (graph features + RF/XGBoost).
Simple fusion (text features + graph features in an MLP).
Full GNN (heterogeneous GAT/GraphSAGE with text node features).
